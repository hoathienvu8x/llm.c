
growing popularity of mix-of-experts (MoE) architectures has led to significant progress in recent years, with models like OpenAI's GShard, Google's Mixtrel and Microsoft's Mixrom, demonstrating that sparse expert routing could achieve competitive or even superior performance compared to traditional dense models with a much smaller parameter count. The key insight lies in the ability to selectively activate and deactivate certain experts, allowing the model to balance between model complexity and inference efficiency.

The OLmoE, or "Optimized Large Language Models with Sparsely Tuned Expert Routing" model provides an interesting point on the spectrum of MoE models, using a relatively small set of 64 expert networks to achieve approximately 1.3 billion active parameters per token (6.9 billion parameters in total), while still managing to take advantage of the increased representational power. This configuration makes it possible to train the model on mass hardware while still being able to benefit from a larger parameter pool.

However, training MoE models present unique challenges, including load balancing across experts, preventing expert collapse, and ensuring stable routing behavior throughout training.
